Why Random Features + PCA Beat Learned Latent Models

A Study of Cross-Subject Imagined Speech Decoding under Small-Sample Constraints

Fixed random features + PCA outperform learned latent representations in cross-subject imagined speech EEG — both pipelines use identical XGBoost classifiers.
→
We demonstrate that increasing representation complexity can harm cross-subject generalization in EEG decoding. A fixed random-feature pipeline (no training · PCA(32) · XGBoost) consistently outperforms a trained DAE with adversarial subject-invariance — using the same classifier. In small-sample EEG, stability and regularization matter more than model capacity.
Motivation
1
Learned representation ≠ better cross-subject generalization — high-capacity models overfit to subject-specific variance under small LOSO sample counts.
2
Channel reduction effects are model-dependent — PCA pipeline is channel-agnostic; P1+DAE benefits from richer input (trend-level, p=0.089).
3
PCA can regularize, not just compress — at C=10, PCA(32) outperforms the uncompressed 1152→XGB upper bound (+0.42pp), acting as implicit denoiser.
Pipeline Comparison
Why does random init work?

Fixed CNN+LSTM acts as a random projection into 1152-d — analogous to random kitchen sink / NTK-regime behavior. Architecture inductive bias (spatial covariance + temporal dynamics) is sufficient without gradient updates.

Explicit representation learning may be unnecessary when random projections already induce a sufficiently expressive feature space — PCA then extracts the most compressible linear subspace.

Discussion
  • A
    Why P1+DAE underperforms: Sensitive to supervision noise and inter-subject variance. Limited per-fold sample count amplifies overfitting. GRL may suppress phonological signal alongside subject-specific variance — a known failure mode of gradient-based domain invariance. t-SNE of actual DAE latent (Fig B) confirms this: task colors are fully mixed while subject-homogeneous subclusters persist, indicating GRL failed to remove subject structure while simultaneously destroying task-discriminative information.
  • B
    Channel effects are architecture-dependent: P1+DAE shows trend-level benefit at C=64 (+1.25pp, p=0.089). PCA baseline is channel-agnostic (Δ=−0.04pp, p=0.879). Paper10 subset provides a more compression-friendly space for PCA.
  • C
    Bias-variance framing: Fixed features exhibit higher bias, lower variance. In N≈14 LOSO folds, lower variance wins (dz=2.07 at C=10). 5-seed stability (σ ≤ 0.0022) confirms a systematic property, not a favorable initialization.
  • !
    Limitations: Single dataset (Kara-One) — generalizability to other benchmarks unverified. No multiple comparison correction applied. Component ablations (PCA alone, random CNN alone) not conducted. Classifier was not ablated (LR vs XGBoost).
  • ?
    Open questions: Does PCA baseline hold with more data? Would a linear classifier (LR) match XGBoost here? Does this generalize to BCI-IV, BNCI, or other imagined speech corpora?
Representation learning failure mode

This reveals a limitation in small-sample EEG: increased model complexity can reduce generalization. A simple, strongly-regularized pipeline constitutes a more reliable baseline — challenging the assumption that learned representations confer cross-subject generalization benefit.

Contribution

We demonstrate that, in small-sample cross-subject EEG decoding, increasing representation complexity can harm generalization, and that simple, strongly regularized methods form a more reliable baseline. Both pipelines share an identical XGBoost classifier — the difference is representation only. Effect size (dz=2.07, p<0.001) and seed stability (σ < 0.003) support this as a robust within-dataset finding requiring multi-dataset replication to establish broader scope.

Conclusion
A fixed-feature pipeline (random CNN+LSTM → PCA(32) → XGBoost) consistently outperforms a trained DAE with adversarial subject-invariance — using the same XGBoost classifier in both cases. The PCA baseline exhibits narrow seed variance and near-zero channel sensitivity. PCA operates as near-lossless compression at C=64 and an implicit regularizer at C=10. In the small-sample cross-subject EEG regime, stability and regularization matter more than model capacity. Multi-dataset replication is required to establish the scope of this finding beyond Kara-One.
